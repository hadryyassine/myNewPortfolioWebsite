If I could summarize this blog in one sentence: never say no to any appealing opportunity. If I had stayed in my little comfort zone, I would not be able to share this story today. This is not a motivational post; I am simply sharing my experience, with both its good and bad parts.

At 15 years old, I was sitting in my French class, waiting for the session to end, when people from the Anoual association entered our classroom. They spoke about an entrepreneurship program for high school students called Tamkeen Initiative. The funny thing is that, back then, I thought an entrepreneur was someone who builds houses, until they said: "You'll have the mission to solve a significant problem in education with innovation and creativity." Those words stayed in my head. I always wanted to make a real change, and this sounded like the right opportunity. So, fortunately, I said yes.

The journey started there. After many workshops and training sessions on design thinking, business models, and business innovation, we had to brainstorm an innovative idea to solve an educational problem. We were 15-year-old kids full of imagination and energy, always looking for fun and adventure. But studies often felt dull; we were in a cycle where we studied only to pass exams. So my team and I started with one question: how can we make studying fun and valuable at the same time?

![Team discussion during the early startup phase](/blog/startup-kids-journey-1.jpeg)

One of our best childhood memories was listening to stories from our grandmothers. We still remembered every detail: Ali Baba, Sindibad, and many more. We were focused, and we enjoyed every minute. That inspired one reflection: why not transform long, boring lessons into fun and memorable stories?

That was the start of Funtawn, a startup creating alternative educational resources through storytelling, cartoons, and mind mapping. Our first product was called Ami Rahal, a comic whose main character was an elderly man named Rahal, who traveled with his grandkids to different worlds where they explored knowledge. They began in the late 1930s, during World War II, meeting different characters, each one representing a country. Then we expanded into lessons like atoms, electric current, and the definition of philosophy. We used metaphors, funny jokes, and caricatures to make lessons more enjoyable, and we ended each chapter with a mind map for better memorization.

![Educational storytelling product examples from Funtawn](/blog/startup-kids-journey-2.jpeg)

After many sleepless nights, obstacles, and hard work, we were proud to win both the regional and national Tamkeen Initiative competition in 2017 and to be titled the best young project in Morocco. We did not stop there. We kept building, while also facing our biggest challenge: balancing school and running a startup.

We managed to reach more than 3,000 students directly and indirectly, ranked among the top 60 projects in Africa by the Anzisha program, and received coverage from outlets like Al Jazeera, Medi1TV, Aswat Magharibia, and L'Economiste. We were invited to entrepreneurship events to share our experience and inspire others. At the same time, we kept strong academic results in high school.

This story was not all roses and candy. We struggled with bureaucracy, complex legal issues, and people who saw us only as kids and tried to exploit us. Then the COVID-19 period became the major turning point. We could not sustain the project through that difficult phase. On top of that, each team member moved to a different college in different cities, and the team spirit naturally declined. We eventually closed that chapter with a big smile and no regret.

I gained a lot from that experience. It changed me personally and professionally. I discovered my passions, strengths, and weaknesses. I left my comfort zone and moved beyond the shy version of myself. I also met great people, especially my colleagues Chaimae and Imane, and expanded my network. As we always said, your network is your net worth. Along with many soft and hard skills, one thing became clear: managing a startup at 15 is not easy.

That is why I never regret saying yes. It was life-changing, and I look forward to new adventures where I can make new mistakes, learn from them, fail, and then succeed.

This is the power of opportunities. Say yes to experiences that can transform you, learn to love change, and seek discomfort.
